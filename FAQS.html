Hawkins v. Kroger Co.,

Case No. 3:15-cv-2320-JM-AHG

U.S. District Court for the Southern District of California

Frequently Asked Questions

The lawsuit claims that Kroger violated federal and California law by marketing and labeling Kroger Bread Crumbs as containing “0g Trans Fat” when they unlawfully contained trans fat. Kroger denies any wrongdoing and asserts that the formulation and labeling of Kroger Bread Crumbs did not violate any law. The Court in charge of the case is the United States District Court for the Southern District of California, and the case is known as Hawkins v. The Kroger Company, Case No. 3:15-cv02320-JM-AHG. The Court has not yet decided whether Plaintiff or Defendant is correct.

In a class action lawsuit, one or more people, called class representatives (in this case Shavonda Hawkins) represent the interests of similarly situated people who have the same claims, but have not filed a lawsuit. Together these people are a class. The person who filed the lawsuit is called the plaintiff and class representative. The company they sue is called the defendant. One court resolves the issues for everyone in the class – except for those people who choose to exclude themselves from the class.

An individual who purchased Kroger Bread Crumbs has sued Defendant to recover money for herself and other people who bought Kroger Bread Crumbs. On November 9, 2020, the Court allowed the case to proceed as a class action for buyers of Kroger Bread Crumbs in California between 2010 and 2015. All these people are a Class or Class Members. The Court will resolve the issues in the case for all Class Members, except for those who choose to exclude themselves from the Class. The Honorable Jeffrey T. Miller is the federal district court judge assigned to the case.

More information about why the Court is allowing this lawsuit to be a class action is in the Order Granting Plaintiff’s Motion for Class Certification available under the “Court Documents” tab.

You are a part of the class if you are a citizen of California who purchased, between January 1, 2010 and December 31, 2015, Kroger Bread Crumb containing partially hydrogenated oil and the front label claim "0g Trans Fat." The product must have been purchased for personal or household use and not for resale or distribution.

You are not a part of the settlement class if you are (1) any judicial officer presiding over the lawsuit, (2) officers and directors of Kroger, or (3) any person who properly executes and files a timely request for exclusion.

Plaintiff is seeking to recover money for herself and the Class based on her claim that the “0g Trans Fat” claim on Kroger Bread Crumbs was false, deceptive, or unlawful, and caused Plaintiff to purchase a product she would not otherwise have purchased. Defendant claims that Plaintiff should recover no money because it has not violated the law. When the case was filed, the Plaintiff also wanted Kroger to stop selling Kroger Bread Crumbs made with partially hydrogenated oil. Plaintiff is no longer seeking this relief because Kroger no longer does this.

No money is available now because the Court has not yet decided whether Defendant has done anything wrong, and the two sides have not settled the case. There is no guarantee that any money will ever be available. If it is, you will be notified about your rights regarding any recovery.

If you do nothing now you will remain a member of the Class. If you stay in the Class and the Plaintiff obtains benefits, either as a result of a trial or a settlement, you will be notified about how to seek a share of those benefits. If you do nothing, you will be bound by the Court’s orders and will lose any right you may have to sue Defendant over the claims in this case. You will also be legally bound by all of the Orders the Court issues and judgments the Court makes in the Class Action, including any judgment against Plaintiff and in favor of Defendant.

If you would like to pursue your own lawsuit or claim against Kroger for the claims in this case, do not want to be bound by what the Court does in this case, or if you simply do not want to be part of the Class pursuing claims against Kroger, you need to ask to be excluded from the Class. If you exclude yourself from the Class – which also means to remove yourself from the Class, and is sometimes called “opting‐out” of the Class – you will not be legally bound by the Court’s judgments in this Class Action, and will keep any right you may have to later sue Defendant for the claims being made in the case. If you exclude yourself, you also will not get any benefits from this lawsuit if there are any.

To ask to be excluded, you must send an “Exclusion Request” in the form of a letter sent by mail to Kroger Bread Crumbs Class Action Litigation, c/o The Weston Firm, 1405 Morena Blvd., Suite 201, San Diego, CA 92110. Your “Exclusion Request” should include your full legal name and address. To be valid, your letter asking to be excluded from the Class must be signed and postmarked no later than [date 90 days after notice]. If there is a settlement, you may have an additional opportunity to exclude yourself rather than accept the settlement

The Court has appointed Gregory Weston of the Weston Firm as Class Counsel in this case. The Court has determined that the Weston Firm is qualified to represent you and all other Class Members. More information about the law firm is available at www.westonfirm.com.

If Class Counsel recovers any money for the Class, they will ask the Court for payment of their fees and reimbursement of their litigation costs. You will not have to pay these fees and expenses out of your own pocket. If the Court grants Class Counsel’s request, the fees and expenses might be deducted from any money obtained for the Class or paid separately by Defendant.
